### At a glance

This comparison looks at two common residential heating approaches:
- **Radiant heat** (hydronic floor, panel radiators, radiant ceilings)
- **Forced-air heating** (ducted air delivery)

Both can heat a home effectively, but they differ in comfort delivery, system interaction, and lifecycle performance.

**Core idea:**  
Radiant heat warms people and surfaces.  
Forced air warms air.

---

### Lifecycle Impact (LIS)

**Radiant systems**
- Higher upfront material and labor (tubing, manifolds, controls)
- Very low distribution losses
- Pair exceptionally well with low-temperature heat sources (heat pumps, condensing boilers)

**Forced-air systems**
- Lower initial cost and fast response
- Duct losses can be significant if poorly designed or routed
- Often combined with cooling, simplifying system count

**Operational efficiency**
- Radiant systems operate at lower water temperatures, improving heat pump efficiency
- Forced air can require higher supply temperatures to feel comfortable

> **LIS takeaway:**  
> Radiant systems often deliver **lower lifecycle energy use** when paired with efficient heat sources and good envelopes.

---

### Regenerative Impact (RIS)

**Comfort quality**
- Radiant heat provides even temperatures and warm surfaces
- Reduced stratification and fewer drafts
- Comfort at lower thermostat setpoints

**Indoor air quality**
- Radiant systems do not move air or dust
- Forced air requires good filtration and maintenance to maintain IAQ

**Durability & adaptability**
- Radiant tubing can last decades
- Forced-air systems are easier to modify but rely on ongoing duct integrity

> **RIS takeaway:**  
> Radiant heat supports calmer, quieter, and more comfortable interiors with fewer moving parts in living spaces.

---

### Cost & CPI (Cost Per Impact)

**Up-front cost**
- Radiant systems cost more initially, especially in retrofits
- Forced air is usually the least expensive to install

**Operating cost**
- Radiant systems can reduce energy use through lower setpoints
- Forced air may increase fan energy and duct losses

**Best value cases**
- Radiant shines in airtight, well-insulated homes
- Forced air makes sense where cooling is dominant or budgets are tight

> **CPI takeaway:**  
> Radiant heat often delivers **higher comfort per dollar** over time, while forced air wins on first cost and flexibility.

---

### Summary Takeaway

- **Radiant heat**
  - Superior comfort
  - Quiet operation
  - Best paired with good envelopes and heat pumps

- **Forced air**
  - Lower upfront cost
  - Faster response
  - Easier integration with cooling and ventilation

**Builder's rule of thumb:**  
> *Radiant is about comfort. Forced air is about convenience. Choose based on the envelope and expectations.*
